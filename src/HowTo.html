ERYA – Profiling : How to make a Profiling Simulation


This section will detail a brief resume of the physical theory of ERYA-Profiling, and a simple guided tutorial to operate the software.

Notice that a complete tutorial manual are available separately.


Yield Evaluation Procedure


The ERYA application will evaluate the yield of several isotopes defined from the user, taking the user-defined parameters and all related databases to calculate the pretended result.

Since the sample are composed by several layers, each one with a define depth in terms of atomic surface density (in 1015 atoms per square centimeter units) and the relative stoichiometry of the present elements on it, the yield evaluation on ERYA-Profiling is more complex than on ERYA-Bulk.


1. Each Yield are related to the element, like on the ERYA-Bulk, but the original integral now becomes a multidimensional integral, since:


Y (E0) = εabs(Eγ)Np NAV ∫ fm(E) fi (E)σ(E)/dε dE


† εabs(Eγ), is the Detector Efficiency to the respective gamma emission energy of the current isotope. Any isotope on ground-level will have a zero gamma yield emission.


† Np is the number of protons calculated from the “Charge” specified from the user (default is 1 µC).


† fm relates to the mass fraction of the isotope related to the sample layer it belongs.

- This means, if the element are alone, their mass fraction will be equal to one.

- If the isotope belongs to a certain compound, the mass fraction is evaluated by taking the weight average of their isotope mass and stoichiometric ratio of current compound.

- Thus, taking the sum, S = Σ sk Ak , the mass fraction will be fm = si Ai / S.


† fi is simply the isotope abundance, and it should be available on the Element Database.


† Nav is the Avogadro Number (≈6.022*1023).


† σ(E) is the Element cross-section function, usually interpolated from the Element’s cross-section data, but can be overridden by a custom resonance function, as explained here.


† The dε quantity is the Energy Stopping loss which use the Bragg's Law and the Ziegler's ansatz equations or SRIM tables to model the energy beam losses.


2. In order to simplify the Yield Calculation, the initial integral are transformed by a sum of partial yields for each elementary layer step, giving:

Y (E0) = ∑ YL (EL)

YL (EL)= εabs(Eγ)Np NAVfmL fiL (σ(EL)/ε(EL) ) ∆EL

∆EL ≈ ε(EL) ΓL

Where Γ is the atomic density at the current layer, and E is the average energy at the same layer.


To model more accurately the energy loss when the incident beam propagates along the sample, the cross-section value should be handled by a distribution integral:

σ(EL) = ∫ F (E) σ (E) dE

The distribution can be understood by a composition of two components:

- The own Detector had a thermal and resolution distribution due to the finite temperature and energy resolution dispersion.

- While beam propagation own energy loss across the sample are described by a propagation distribution, also called straggling distribution, since it is related to the Energy Stopping Loss.

† The global distribution is a convolution of the two distributions previously discussed:

F (E ) = ∫ FT (F) FS (E – F ) dF


Where the thermal FT is always a Gaussian Distribution, and the straggling FS are a Vavilov distribution, where such distribution requires special routines to handle at best computation time.

This means that the evaluation of the cross-section are handled by a numerical double integral, using the Simpson's Rule, giving by:

σ(EL) = ∫∫ FT (y) FS (x-y) σ(EL-x) dx dy


Combining the whole distribution integral of the cross-section relative by their layer, the whole yield numerical integral is now a triple numerical integral, making the numerical evaluation more time consuming in clear contrast of the simple integration on the ERYA-Bulk.


3. The program will compute an array of several yields, ranging from the user selected Energy Minimum, until the Maximum Energy, by equally increasing initial energies defined by the Energy Step. The sample is also divided by smaller layers, using a default Sample Step of 10*1015 at/cm2 unit, where can be changed, by clicking on “Advanced” button on the bottom interface.


Distributions


The ERYA-Profiling use a custom numerical library, in order to evaluate the Vavilov distribution using the original references, first derived from Montagna R. work and Luis F. thesis work that was also the previous LabView ERYA-Profiling author.

Currently, all numerical routines on ERYA-Profiling was reimplemented from scratch.


For each initial energy at any layer EL, the program evaluate the relativistic velocity β , the energy cut-off κMAX , the resolution factor ξ , and the factor k = ξ / κMAX .

Once the factors β, k, ξ are evaluated, it also evaluates the Bohr variance, requiring to use the Mean Ionization Energy, and Density, located on the Stopping-Power file.

Finally, the straggling variance can be evaluated by the following formula, derived by H. Bichsel:

σB2 = (8 ξ) / (3 Γ) ∑ si Ii ln ( 2 me β2 / Ii )


But the user can change the straggling variance formula to use the Vavilov formula instead, by clicking the “Advanced” button:

σV2 = ξ2 * ( 1 - β2 / 2 ) / k


A final option to force a Gaussian distribution to the Straggling Distribution (which is fairly accurate for bulk samples), will defaults the Vavilov formula to the straggling variance.


To reduce numerical errors during the whole complex chain of numerical steps, the program will evaluate each partial factor β, k, ξ, σB2 along each layer and sum such factors along the previous values. The accumulated factors are the main inputs for the numerical Vavilov Distribution, and applied according on the following rules, if the user don’t force a pure Gaussian Distribution:


For k=0, the Vavilov Distribution is a degenerate one, a single point handled by a Dirac’s Function.


For 0<k<0.02, the Vavilov Distribution can be approximated by a Landau Function.


For 0.02<k<0.24 are on domain of the Moyal Function, described by Montagna R. work on numerical approximations of the Vavilov Distributions.


For 0.24<k<22 applies the Airy Function, where the distribution maximum will slowly converge to the Gaussian peak.


For 22<k<25 the Vavilov Distribution are approximated by the Edgeworth Function, which is a small correction of the Gaussian Distribution.


Finally for k>25, the Vavilov Distribution can be approximate by the Gaussian Function.


The thermal distribution is always a Gaussian Distribution, unless the degenerate case when the temperature and resolution are zero (an impossible ideal detector), making a Dirac’s Function.


Note: The ERYA-Profiling approximate all distributions by a 3-sigma cut-off, since it won’t make sense an unlimited interval on numerical integration. Except the Dirac’s Function, that use a single point, the remaining functions accuracy can be changed on “Advanced” button, located on the bottom main interface.

Due to the limiting range domain, all distributions should be renormalized, and where the program actually makes two double integrals, fortunately with the same domain matrix, and divide them to obtain the renormalized value:

σ(EL) = ∫∫ FT (y) FS (x-y) σ(EL-x) dx dy / ∫∫ FT (y) FS (x-y) dx dy


Profiling Analysis How-To


1. You need to check first if you have a functional database. If the program warns about an invalid database for Elements, Stopping-Power or Detector Setup, you need to check the source database files (You should put all three in the same directory), or change the default configuration, as explained on “Settings” menu option.

Alternatively, use the menu option “Settings”>”Reset Settings” to reset the wrong configuration.

2. You can override the default Element Database loaded at start-up, using the adequate menu.

† Tip: Also check the Detector Setup and Stopping-Power from the appropriate menu options.


3. The first step is to use the “Elements” tab, and select the number of Elements, and their names and gammas from the main database. The other tabs contents are automatically updated.


4. The sample composition are defined on “Layer” tab, first by selecting the number of layers, and finally, fill the depth, and the relative stoichiometry for each element. During the numerical evaluation, the stoichiometry for each layer are internally renormalized without additional user input.


5. Finally, on the “Detector” tab, select the physical parameters of the Detector, and an additional custom resonance, if needed.


6. Once everything complete, click “Run Simulation” on the bottom line of the main program interface. The program will check all inputs, and build the necessary memory structures to ensure a smooth numerical evaluation.

On the status bar, it will display a progress percentage during the numerical computations. In real samples, it may take several minutes, or even hours (depending the complexity of the sample) to complete the process, and the progress bar information is the main indication that the program are working as expected.


In Case of an Error: If an invalid option (non-numerical input, negative values for Energy, etc), or any syntax error occurs, the main numerical procedure are halted, and return an error, warning the user when (at best possible) the first fatal error was found.

The user should make the necessary corrections, and try again.


Changing Precision Parameters: Click on “Advanced” button, and change the values, in order to balance the precision, and fast evaluation.


7. Save the all made samples, using “File” > “Save”, and will save the Elements, Layers, Detector and Resonances data, along the custom parameters on a XML file with epgs extension.

When needed, just load the same file from “File” > “Open”, to avoid manual typing of all parameters again.


8. Once the main procedure is completed. The results are displayed on the last three tabs: a graphic plot of the yields spectrum on “Output” tab, where is also possible to add additional data on the plot without deleting the previous ones, unlike the numerical procedure that erase all plot data. A numeric table from the same yields, are also displayed, along a full log of all partial numerical calculations.

The numerical table, and log table can be saved to an ASCII or Excel Xlsx file directly by clicking on the buttons “Save Table” and “Save Log” located on the bottom of the main program interface.
