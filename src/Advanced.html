ERYA – Profiling Advanced Settings



Normally, ERYA-Profiling will handle conveniently the numerical tasks to provide quickly the tangible results. In some rare occasions, it may be necessary to change some system parameters, in order to manually tunning the balance between the accuracy and the computation time.


Understanding the Parameters:

The eight changeable parameters are the following in order:


1 (Sample Step):

The most important and relevant numerical parameter, where it define the numerical integral step by the sample size. ERYA assume that the sample are equally sliced by a fixed amount of atoms, where the user can choose between 1 to 1000*1015 at.cm2 (number of atoms per square centimeter). This means that all layers sizes defined on Layer Editor tab are sliced even further by the quantity selected by this parameter. To increase accuracy, select the smaller number possible, but the computation time will increase porportionally.

To balance the precision and computation time, make a simulation as described here, and look at the column “Energy Loss” from Log, where it is possible and make an average guess of how many energy is lost where the beam pass along a single material slice.

Normally, a energy loss of about 1 keV is good enough for many realistic samples, unless some samples with sharp resonances may require more precision.

For thin samples, a default 10*1015 at.cm2 is adequate. Bulk samples, or with smooth cross-sections may be handled with a bigger sample step, without great loss of accuracy.


2 (Gauss), 3 (Moyal), 4 (Airy), 5 (Edgewoth), 6 (Landau Step):

The next five parameters are related to the numerical approximation of the Vavilov Distribution.

In order to achieve a reasonable numerical method, the Vavilov Distribution are computed by four different distributions, depending from the Parameter k (As explained on Log Output tab).

All computations are trunked to the 3-sigma approximation (Also known as L=99.5%), and renormalized. A simple conversion between the Energy (E) and Landau Variable (λ), defined as:

λ = (E – ΔE)/ξ – 1 + γ – β2 - ln(k)

E = ξ (λ + 1 – γ + β2 + ln(k)) + ΔE

Where β is the relativistic velocity obtaining from the Einstein’s formulas (even the energies from proton beams are non-relativistic), γ≈0.577 is the Euler’s constant, k are obtained from internal calculations from the sample composition, and ΔE are related to the energy straggling.

The Landau, Moyal, Airy and Edgeworth Distributions 3-sigma domains can be placed between λ=-3.4 and λ=10 to 25 (It depends from the parameter k). Is possible for the user select how any points this interval can be sliced evenly. The Moyal Distribution is an exception, since it will ask how an unitary Landau variable interval can be sliced.


The standard values are made to achieve a Landau Step of about δλ=0.1. If the user select to increase the default values, the Landau Step are smaller, but it will take more time to evaluate the distribution.

In reality the program use the Energy Step, defined by δE = ξ δλ, since it is the real physical quantity on all numerical calculations.

The last distribution, is the Gauss Distribution, related to the thermal and resolution distribution and the straggling distribution for k>25, are adjustable to the default 3-sigma domain range, selecting the number of steps that this interval can be evenly divided. The average of Gaussian are the ΔE value for the straggling distribution, and zero for the thermal and resolution; while the standard-deviation are evaluated from the sample composition.


7 (Bohr/Vavilov/Gauss Limit):

The next relevant parameter changes the physical model used when the Vavilov Distribution at higher parameter-k values are indistinguishable from the Gaussian Distribution. The user can choose between the Vavilov Limit or the Bohr-Bichsel formula, defined as the follows:


σV2 = ξ2 *(1 – β2/2) / k


σB2 = ( 8 ξ ) / ( 3 x ) ∑ I * ln ( 2 me v2 / I )


Where the last formula are dependent of the mean ionization I , the sample depth x , and the kinetic energy of incident beam. All those values are stored from the Ziegler’s Parameters file.


A third option (Gaussian) will override the Vavilov distribution to the Gaussian one to the straggling distribution, ignoring the parameter-k values. This option is only useful for bulk samples, but had the advantage to reduce the computation time by a factor of hundreds compared to the Vavilov distributions, and the yields results are almost the same.


8 (Enable/Disable Log):

Select the check-box to enable the Log Output tab spreadsheet table, or uncheck to bypass the logging. For huge samples, disable the log will save several minutes of computation time, and display the yields a little more fast.

Normally, the Logging are only useful for a reduced number of elementary steps, in order to deduce the pattern for large samples.
